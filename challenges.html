Challenges

Newspaper clipping



Like all movements, the ACJ faced many challenges in their movement. The media that had proved so useful to the ACJ earlier in starting the federal investigation also was the media that allowed Ronald Ebens to win his appeal on retrial. His main claims were that the trial failed in allowing him to change the venue, as Detroit had very prominent and intense coverage and publicity that was very scathing. Ebens argued that the jury was biased and had prior knowledge through the media.He also argued that the trial failed when excluding testimony on grounds of hearsay from Liza Chan’s interview tapes. The court agreed with Ebens, allowing for his appeal and retrial to become successful.


Sign in the auto industry showing the anti-Japanese sentiment at the time from imported cars.


The topic of race was another challenge the ACJ faced. Many Asian Americans were unsure about speaking out against injustice, especially the immigrant, refugee, and elder communities. Many Asian Americans were raised with the model minority myth –- the idea that they should just keep their head down and work hard. However, they eventually decided that they were going to talk about race and advocate for Asian Americans. When they made this decision public, there was immediate backlash. There was sentiment that Asian Americans didn’t experience “real” racism or discrimination and no place when discussing race. Race was viewed as a black/white issue at the time, and people were unsure if the civil rights law even included Asian Americans. Even Ebens himself said that he didn’t know there was such a thing as an “Asian American plight,” even though anti-Japanese sentiment was clearly evident during the WWII incarceration and the targeting of Japanese cars and people. This made it more challenging for the ACJ to prove that Chin’s murder was racially motivated and that his civil rights were violated if people did not believe that Asian Americans could be discriminated against or were included in the civil rights laws. Similarly, there was sentiment that Asians were trying to “ride the coattails” of Black people and their movements, while pointing out that many Asians themselves have anti-Black prejudice. The ACJ was built upon previous Asian American movements like Yellow Power, which was heavily influenced by the Black Power movement, and the ACJ acknowledged their influence from Black civil rights movements. Previous civil rights movements helpd inform the nonviolent strategies the ACJ used, and the student activism that helped coin the term 'Asian American' was also important in laying the foundations for this movement. Additionally, the ACJ tried to educate Asian Americans about the civil rights movements and committed to supporting Black communities to build solidarity.


Though coalition building was central and important in the ACJ’s success in building national and federal support, it proved very challenging. There was fighting within the organization as people of different class, language, culture, and political backgrounds came together. To help prevent this, the ACJ prohibited talking about international affairs and refused international aid to keep building solidarity among otherwise polarized groups, including the fervent nationalists and communist supporters. Despite these efforts, conflicts still occurred, like the highly publicized event in San Francisco Chinatown's Cameron House, where the local rival political factions tried elbowing each other off stage.
